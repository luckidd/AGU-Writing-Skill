# Claim-Evidence Review

## Evidence chain

For each major claim, identify:

`claim -> direct evidence -> physical inference -> plausible alternatives -> boundary`

Use these evidence labels when a table is useful:

- **Direct:** directly measured, calculated, or produced by the stated analysis.
- **Supported inference:** requires one defensible interpretive step.
- **Suggestive:** multiple explanations remain.
- **Unsupported:** missing evidence, condition, uncertainty, or citation.

Suggested table: `Claim | Evidence | Level | Alternatives | Boundary or revision`.

## Paragraph test

Each paragraph should have one job and normally progress through:

`topic claim -> evidence -> interpretation -> boundary -> transition`

Use a reverse outline. Merge duplicate paragraph jobs; move material that does not support the section objective.

## Competing-explanation checks

- Correlation versus causation: is there timing, mechanism, intervention, or independent diagnostic support?
- Temporal versus spatial structure: can spacecraft motion or sampling explain the apparent change?
- Observation versus model assumption: which result is measured, derived, prescribed, or simulated?
- Adiabatic versus nonadiabatic response: can field or coordinate changes explain flux variation?
- Local process versus transport: are gradients, phase-space density, timing, or spatial context sufficient to distinguish them?
- Instrument response versus physical signal: are sensitivity, contamination, calibration, cadence, and coverage addressed?
- Forecast skill versus fit: is performance compared with a meaningful baseline on independent data, with failures and operating constraints stated?

Do not force every alternative into the prose. Mention alternatives that materially change the conclusion and state which diagnostic supports or fails to exclude them.

## Final review

- Major claims map to figures, tables, calculations, or verified citations.
- Quantitative claims retain ranges, regimes, and uncertainties supplied by the author.
- Mechanism statements do not exceed the diagnostics.
- Single events or limited samples are not generalized without qualification.
- Results and Discussion remain distinguishable.
- Missing facts remain explicit placeholders; templates never supply evidence.
